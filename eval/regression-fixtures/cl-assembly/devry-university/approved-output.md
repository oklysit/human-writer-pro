One thing I've been thinking about as a student is the lack of speed with which education has adopted AI. People are already defaulting to asking ChatGPT a question. It's sort of the new Google. WGU right now uses Turnitin, which is nowhere near the most effective AI-detecting tool. It may be better for plagiarism, so it might detect verbatim-from-source stuff. But plenty of students like me are much more capable of creating personalized tools that make their lives easier and may be really difficult, if not impossible, to detect. So the real question is this: should we be putting more effort into detecting them, or more effort into tailoring how they learn with these new tools?

I built CertBuddy (a RAG study tool) because I wanted to customize exactly how I learn with my own methods. I have the AI generate practice tests off chunked training material. There's flashcards to drill concepts and acronyms, and anything I need to internalize gets sourced from actual training materials. Usually within a couple of days I'm ready to take a certification -- part of how I'm on track to finish 18 courses this WGU term. AI tools ease the mental load quite a bit, the same way calculators did for mental math. For things that matter, we still need critical thinking. For day-to-day stuff, the tradeoff is fine.

Here's what I've figured out about being good at AI tool use: it's communication-forward. People who get better outputs tend to be strong empathizers. They think about what the LLM needs to understand in order to do what you want it to do. Not everyone is a strong communicator, and not everyone thinks like that. That skill is what I have, and it's quite honestly why I built tools instead of grinding syntax.

On the gap side: no Microsoft Copilot Studio, no Salesforce GPT. My closest enterprise exposure is Glean. The gap is real, but so is my learning velocity, let's say.

The ideal person is communication-forward and curious enough to experiment. That's exactly what I am.
