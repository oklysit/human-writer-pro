And not so much for improving their own workflows or anything, but for understanding that people are using it, right? That it's sort of the new Google and people are defaulting to asking ChatGPT a question. And not only do people gravitate towards these tools nowadays, you have to understand a lot more about how they learn using these tools. There's plenty of studies showing, you know, there's some advantages, but also some disadvantages in the way that people learn. we have to, I think, experiment and figure out like what's what what exactly is best for the students and how to advise faculty about that. So one of the things, for example, is WGU right now uses Turnitin. And you know, correct me if I'm wrong here, but Turnitin, I believe is nowhere near the most effective AI detecting tool. It may be better for plagiarism. So it might detect AI output that is verbatim from some study or something like that. But you know, outside of that, I just don't think it's that effective a tool that they could or should be using. And then going beyond that, even if they were using a very effective tool, my projects have shown that there's ways around it, and there's ways to customize how the text is output and whether or not an automated system can detect it. It's definitely true that there's students that are using AI to just write their essays, in a single shot prompt, but there's definitely also plenty of students like myself who are much more capable of creating something that'll make their life easier and personalized and may be really difficult, if not impossible to detect. And when you do that, you have to realize, well, how do we support these students? What is it that they want to learn, or how much are they losing by doing this? Should we be putting more effort towards detecting them, or should we be putting more effort towards tailoring how they learn with these new tools? And that requires a very move fast approach, and I think that is what appeals to me for sure.

## Q2 (CertBuddy2 motivation + technical narrative + philosophical frame) — answered 2026-04-12

So the big thing that led me to make the CertBuddy thing was kind of efficiency. I think for me, I've realized that self-directed learning was much better than sitting and listening to a lecture. I often found myself throughout high school and college realizing, okay, we're doing a 45 minute lecture for something that is essentially two to three pages of material that I feel at least for myself and for many others, you could have grasped fairly quickly and moved on to application and teaching and figuring out a way to really internalize these concepts. It can be frustrating to learn in that kind of traditional environment. So this sort of self-directed learning and online schools specifically are very compatible with the way that I learn. And AI tools are sort of an evolution of that.

I realized, what are the best ways that I pass certifications? I often find myself just looking at videos — which is kind of ironic because I don't like to listen to a 45 minute lecture, but if you look at video tutorials or video courses, they're often very condensed. You can watch within a couple of days the material you need, listen at 1.5x speed, grasp quite a bit of that — at least for the purposes of passing a test. The reason I think this is helpful is because I actually don't think passing these tests is necessarily the best way to internalize concepts. I think it's about application. I'm very opinionated on the way that things should move forward. I think we should return to apprenticeships and these tools just really let you leverage critical thinking in very focused bursts.

I built the tool because I wanted to customize exactly how I learn with my own methods. If I have a RAG database, I can ask questions that synthesize from an entire textbook. My original preferred way was to straight-read 800 pages or listen to a 70 hour video course series, and that was how I would pass certifications faster than anything else. I thought I could do better if I simply was able to ask. My new methodology: take practice tests, have the AI generate practice tests off the chunked training material, talk to it. Flashcards for drill and acronyms. For anything I really need to internalize, I have it sourced from actual training materials, and I pick it up much faster. I can go through a bunch of practice tests, come back, ask some questions to make sure I understand the concepts, and usually within a couple of days I'm ready to take a certification. That's part of how I'm on track to finish 18 courses this WGU term (9 certs total expected).

Does that make me totally qualified? Not necessarily, but I don't think most recent graduates are the most qualified and capable of working in a real enterprise environment either. That's where soft skills come in. Once you get your foot in the door, that's where a lot of learning really happens — practical application. Part of how education should shift is: people are going to have to specialize and focus and learn how to use these tools. For day-to-day things they'll use AI and turn off critical thinking — just like we turn off our brain for mental calculations, and I think that's okay. For things that matter, we need deliberate critical thinking, ideally cultivated in a technology-free environment so people learn how to ask themselves questions and explore deeply when needed. The juxtaposition we need is: a way of using these AI tools, but then putting them aside and making sure critical thinking still develops. Same way calculators eased the mental load of a lot of tasks. AI tools can ease the mental load of other things. In a world inundated with information and ads, it's okay to take some mental load off.
