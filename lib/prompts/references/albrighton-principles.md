# Albrighton Principles (paraphrased, with source citation)

## 1. Write for the reader, not for yourself
Think about what the reader needs to understand, not what you want to say. The goal is their comprehension, not your self-expression. If the reader can't follow, it's the writer's failure, not theirs.

## 2. Plan before you draft
Know three things before the first sentence: who's your audience, what's your one core message, and in what sequence should it arrive. Drafting without these is guessing.

## 3. Prefer plain language
Shortest word that fits. Shortest sentence that carries the meaning. Jargon only when the audience shares the jargon. "Assist" when "help" works is a failure of respect for the reader's time.

## 4. Make it real with metaphor and story
Abstract concepts land when anchored to concrete images or short narratives. "Trust compounds like interest" says more than a paragraph of abstractions about trust.

## 5. Edit ruthlessly
Cut 30% on the first pass. Cut another 15% on the second pass. What remains earns its place. Most sentences are too long, most paragraphs have too many ideas, most pieces have too many sections.

## Application

- Before drafting: apply #2 — name the audience + message + sequence explicitly
- During drafting: apply #3 — default to shortest word / shortest sentence
- For technical topics: apply #4 — find the metaphor
- During editing: apply #5 — expect big cuts, not small tweaks
